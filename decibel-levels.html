How loud is it, really?

Decibels are hard to picture until you anchor them to sounds you know. This chart maps everyday noise to its typical level, shows where hearing risk begins, and explains why the scale climbs so steeply. When you are ready, point the live decibel meter at the room and see for yourself.

Exposure times follow the NIOSH 3 dB rule. They are a screening guide for awareness, not a substitute for occupational measurement with certified equipment.

Why the scale is logarithmic

The decibel scale is not linear. Every increase of 10 dB represents roughly ten times more sound energy, yet it sounds only about twice as loud to us. So 90 dB is not a little louder than 80 dB, it carries ten times the energy. This compression is the only practical way to handle the enormous range between the faintest sound you can hear and one that causes pain.

It also explains why small numbers matter. Moving from 85 to 88 dB doubles the sound energy reaching your ear, which is exactly why safe exposure time halves with each 3 dB step.

The 85 decibel line

Health authorities draw the safety line at 85 dB over an eight hour day. Below it, ordinary exposure is considered safe for most people. Above it, the clock starts, and the louder it gets the faster damage accumulates. A single very loud event, like fireworks at close range, can harm hearing instantly.

How damage happens

Sound reaches the inner ear and bends tiny hair cells in the cochlea that turn vibration into nerve signals. Loud or prolonged sound overworks and eventually destroys these cells, and unlike skin or bone they do not grow back. The result is permanent noise-induced hearing loss, often first noticed as trouble following speech in a noisy room, and sometimes as a persistent ringing called tinnitus.

Protecting your hearing

Keep everyday exposure below 70 dB where you can, wear protection in environments above 85 dB, give your ears quiet breaks after loud events, and keep headphone volume moderate. A simple habit that helps is to measure first. Knowing a space sits at 95 dB rather than guessing changes how long you choose to stay.
