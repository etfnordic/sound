How to measure sound the right way

Click start and allow microphone access. The big number is your current level; the gauge mirrors it, and the green to red colour tells you which zone you are in at a glance. The spectrum on the right shows which frequencies carry the energy, and the waveform view turns the meter into a simple oscilloscope.

For a measurement you can trust, hold the device still, keep the microphone uncovered, and let it run for thirty to sixty seconds so brief spikes from a cough or a door average out. Compare the Avg Leq figure rather than a single peak when you want to judge a room.

A-weighting versus Z-weighting

Switch to dB(A) for anything to do with hearing safety or noise nuisance. It shapes the reading to match how the ear actually responds, the same weighting used in workplace and environmental standards. Use dB(Z) when you want the raw, flat level across the whole spectrum, for example when chasing low-frequency hum.

Fast or slow response

Real sound level meters average over a short window before showing a number. Fast uses a 125 millisecond window and reacts quickly, which is good for catching transient events. Slow averages over a full second and gives a steadier reading that is easier to compare between rooms or moments.

Why your first reading might surprise you

Phone and laptop microphones differ in sensitivity by ten decibels or more, and browsers normally apply noise reduction and automatic gain that quietly distort levels. This meter switches that processing off so you see the real signal. If you own a reference meter, set a one-time calibration offset and your readings will line up.
