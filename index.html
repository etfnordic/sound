What is a decibel meter?

A decibel meter, also called a sound level meter (SLM), measures the intensity of sound in the air around it. The unit of measurement is the decibel (dB), named after Alexander Graham Bell. Because the human ear can detect an enormous range of pressures — from a pin drop to a jet engine — the decibel scale is logarithmic rather than linear.

A 10 dB increase sounds roughly twice as loud to a human listener. A 20 dB increase represents 100 times the sound pressure. That is why going from a quiet office at 50 dB to a rock concert at 110 dB feels so dramatically different.

This browser-based tool uses the Web Audio API to read raw audio from your microphone, compute the root mean square (RMS) amplitude of each audio frame, and convert the result to a dB SPL reading. The calibration is tuned to typical laptop and smartphone microphones.

How accurate is a browser decibel meter?

Consumer microphones vary considerably in sensitivity, frequency response, and self-noise. Readings from this tool should be treated as useful estimates rather than calibrated measurements. For relative comparisons — "is my bedroom quieter with the window closed?" — browser meters work very well. For regulatory or occupational health purposes, use a certified Class 1 or Class 2 sound level meter.

To get the most consistent readings: hold the device still, keep the microphone uncovered, and measure for at least 30 seconds to smooth out transient spikes.

According to NIOSH guidelines, sustained exposure above 85 dB for 8 hours per day can lead to noise-induced hearing loss. Every additional 3 dB halves the safe exposure time.

Protecting your hearing

Noise-induced hearing loss is permanent. The hair cells in your cochlea that convert sound vibrations into nerve signals do not regenerate once damaged. Wearing hearing protection in loud environments, limiting headphone volume, and monitoring your surroundings with a sound meter are the most effective preventive steps.

Frequently asked questions

No audio is ever recorded, stored, or sent to any server. All processing happens locally in your browser. The microphone stream is read in real time to calculate dB levels and frequencies, then discarded. Only you can access your audio.
Consumer microphones differ in sensitivity. A laptop microphone may read 5 to 15 dB differently from a smartphone in the same room. Environmental factors — wind, room reflections, distance from the source — also shift readings. Use the tool for relative comparisons rather than absolute calibrated values.
All tools rely on the Web Audio API and the getUserMedia API. Chrome, Edge, Firefox, and Safari (14.1 and later on iOS, 14.1 and later on macOS) all support these features. The page must be served over HTTPS for microphone access to work, which is the case here.
The analyzer applies a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to the microphone signal and plots the energy at each frequency bin. Low frequencies (bass) appear on the left, high frequencies on the right. The peak frequency shown is the single strongest component in that moment. This is useful for identifying hum, resonance in a room, or the dominant pitch of a sound source.
The tone generator uses the Web Audio OscillatorNode to produce a mathematically perfect waveform at the selected frequency. Sine waves are smooth and pure, square waves have a hollow electronic sound, triangle waves are softer than square, and sawtooth waves are bright and buzzy. Use it for testing speakers, checking hearing range, or tuning acoustic spaces.
The tapper measures the average interval between your taps and converts it to beats per minute. Accuracy improves with more taps — after 4 to 6 taps you get a stable reading. Human tapping variability is usually within 2 to 3 BPM of the true tempo. For very fast tempos above 180 BPM, tap on every other beat and multiply by 2.
